  • Book, Part 1: Site Map & Content Organization

    Choose an existing piece of text you'd like to work with. It can be a short story, a poem, an essay—anything you'd like to reimagine. Condense the source text to 2–5 pages if necessary.

    Some questions to consider: What are the themes of the text and why did you choose it? How can your concept reflect or challenge those themes? How will you divide the text across pages? How many webpages will you need? How will users navigate the site? How does the meaning of the text shift when presented in a non-linear format?

    The deliverables for this assignment are two-pronged:

    1. Using pen and paper, create a site map: a visualization of the structure and navigation of your website. Think about how the author originally intended the text to be read and how you want to retell the story in this new web medium.
    2. In a text editor of your choice (e.g. Microsoft Word, Google Docs, etc.), divide the text into separate pages based on your site map. As you organize the content, think about hierarchy. How will the text be structured on each page? What headings or subheadings can help guide the reader through the content? Do not think about styling or coding yet—this part of the project is purely focused on content organization and structure.

    If you’re unsure what piece of text to use, you’re welcome to take a look at any of the below resources:

    • Poetry Foundation
    • Project Gutenberg
    • Monoskop
    • Open Library
    • Graphic Design Reading

    This assignment is worth 4 out of 20 points of Project 1: Book. To get full credit, you must hit all of the requirements below.

    2. I chose an existing text to work with.

    3. My site map reflects all of the pages of my website, which has at least five pages.

    4. My site map reflects an interesting way to re-tell the story I’ve chosen.
